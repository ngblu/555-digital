# Cold Call Roleplay Personas — 555 Digital
> Practice against these. Read their lines out loud. Get comfortable deflecting.
> The real people are usually somewhere between these three.

---

## Persona 1: "Ronnie" — The Skeptical Older Tradesman
**Profile:** Ronnie is 55-65, owns a small plumbing/HVAC shop, 3-5 trucks. He's been in business 30 years, built it on reputation. His website was set up by his nephew in 2016 and he hasn't looked at it since. He doesn't trust marketing people — he's been burned by Yellow Pages salesmen, SEO cold callers, and "guys who talk fast." He answers the phone expecting to say no.

**Voice:** Gruff but not mean. Short sentences. Doesn't fill silence. Will interrupt if you sound like a salesman.

**Opening lines (he says these):**
- "Who is this again?"
- "Look, I already got a website. It's fine."
- "I don't do any of that online stuff. My phone rings enough."
- "How'd you get my number?"

**His real concern:** He doesn't want to look stupid. He knows his website is probably outdated but admitting that means admitting he neglected something. He'd rather say "it's fine" than "I don't know how to fix it."

**How to win him over:**
- Don't sell. Show. "Let me show you what your site looks like on a phone real quick."
- Use numbers he feels: "At 8 seconds load time, roughly half your mobile visitors leave before they see your number."
- Respect his experience: "You've built a solid business. The website just needs to catch up to the quality of your work."
- Be direct: "If your site is actually fine, I'll tell you and we hang up. Fair?"

**Budget objection:** He'll say $2,500 is too much. Remind him one emergency plumbing call is $500-800. The site pays for itself in a few calls.

## Persona 2: "Tanya" — The Busy Owner Who Half-Listens
**Profile:** Tanya is 35-45, runs a cleaning or detailing business with employees. She's overwhelmed — managing staff, doing the books, handling customer complaints. Her website is on her to-do list somewhere between "call the insurance guy" and "fix the van's AC." She'll take your call because she knows she should, but her brain is on 12 other things.

**Voice:** Rushed, polite, distracted. Will agree to things she doesn't actually process. Says "uh huh" a lot. Background noise — she's probably in a vehicle or walking through a job site.

**Opening lines (she says these):**
- "Can you send me an email? I'm kind of in the middle of something."
- "Yeah, we definitely need to update the site. I just haven't had time."
- "What's this going to cost? Just give me a range."
- "How long does it take? Like, do I need to do anything?"

**Her real concern:** She knows it needs doing but the mental load of another project feels crushing. She wants someone who will handle it without needing her to make 50 decisions.

**How to win her over:**
- Lead with speed and low-lift: "Two to three weeks, and I need maybe two calls from you total."
- Take things off her plate: "You send me photos and your phone number — I handle the writing, layout, SEO."
- Don't ask open-ended questions — give her options: "Do mornings or afternoons work better for a 10-minute audit call?"
- Follow up fast: "I'll send the audit link right now — open it when you have 2 minutes."

## Persona 3: "Derrick" — The Price Shopper
**Profile:** Derrick is 25-40, runs a newer service business, maybe 1-3 years in. He's tech-aware enough to know he needs a website but not enough to know quality differences. He's already looked at Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy, maybe Fiverr. He sees "website" as a commodity and his instinct is to comparison shop on price. He's not broke — he just doesn't know what he's paying for.

**Voice:** Friendly, confident, maybe a little smug. He thinks he's done his research. Uses phrases like "I was looking at options" and "I saw you can get a site for..."

**Opening lines (he says these):**
- "So what makes your sites better than Wix? Because Wix is like 15 bucks."
- "I talked to a guy on Fiverr who'll do it for $300."
- "What's your cheapest option?"
- "Can you just do the SEO part? I'll build the site myself."
- "I mean, it's just a website, right? Couple pages, some pictures."

**His real concern:** He's not cheap — he's uninformed. He genuinely doesn't understand the difference between a template and a custom site. He thinks "online presence" is a checkbox, not a revenue driver. If you can show him the ROI, the price objection disappears.

**How to win him over:**
- Don't defend price. Show value gap: "The $300 Fiverr site won't have city pages that rank you in Crossville, Cookeville, and Sparta. That's the difference between showing up on page one and being invisible."
- Use his comparison instinct: "Wix is like renting an apartment — you don't own it and you can't change the floorplan. What I build you own. If you want to move it to a different host next year, you can."
- ROI math: "What's one new customer worth to you? My site for a plumber in Cookeville pays for itself in 3-4 jobs."
- Offer the audit as proof: "Let me show you exactly what your current setup is missing. Ten minutes, no pitch. If you can get the same results with Wix, I'll tell you."

**The trap:** Don't negotiate on price in the first call. He's testing you. If you drop your price immediately, he knows you were overcharging. Hold firm on value, then offer payment terms (50/50 split).
